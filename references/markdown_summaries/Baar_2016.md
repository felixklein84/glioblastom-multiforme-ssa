# Source Summary: Baar_2016.pdf

## 1. Bibliographic Information

- **Title:** A stochastic model for immunotherapy of cancer
- **Authors:** Martina Baar, Loren Coquille, Hannah Mayer, Michael Hölzel, Meri Rogava, Thomas Tüting & Anton Bovier
- **Year:** 2016
- **Journal:** Scientific Reports, 6:24169
- **DOI:** 10.1038/srep24169

---

## 2. Why This Source Matters

This paper is the primary mathematical reference for the modeling approach used in the thesis project. It establishes a stochastic individual-based (agent-based) Markov chain model for cancer cell population dynamics that explicitly incorporates phenotypic switching, therapy effects, competition, and mutation. The mathematical framework — a continuous-time Markov chain with a finite trait space, scaled by carrying capacity K — is essentially the same structure as a CTMC and directly comparable to the thesis model. The paper also proves and applies the mean-field ODE limit (K → ∞), demonstrating the standard duality between stochastic and deterministic descriptions that the new project should implement.

## 3. Biological Background

**Primary application:** Melanoma under adoptive cell transfer (ACT) immunotherapy, not GBM. The mathematical framework is general, however, and explicitly designed for transfer to other tumor types.

**Cell types modeled:**
- Differentiated melanoma cells (x): antigen-positive, recognizable by T-cells.
- Dedifferentiated melanoma cells (y): antigen-negative, not recognizable by T-cells.
- T-cells (z): inject to kill differentiated cells.
- TNF-α cytokines (w): trigger phenotypic switching from x to y.

**Phenotypic plasticity:**
- Melanoma cells switch phenotype between differentiated and dedifferentiated states in response to TNF-α (reversible, non-genetic).
- Switching is environment-dependent but does not affect the genotype.
- The switch is the primary driver of therapy failure (immune escape).

**Therapy resistance mechanisms:**
- Phenotypic switching allows cells to escape T-cell therapy.
- Stochastic extinction of the T-cell population during remission leads to relapse with differentiated cells.
- Two-specificity T-cell therapy (targeting both phenotypes) dramatically increases cure probability.

**Competition:**
- A "birth-reducing competition" term is included, which lowers the reproduction rate of cells in the presence of competitors — distinct from competition that only increases death rate. This is a model extension over standard logistic growth.

**Relevance to GBM:**
- Although applied to melanoma, the framework of phenotypic switching, therapy-induced remission, stochastic relapse, and multi-drug strategy directly maps to GBM biology (e.g., GSC/progenitor transitions, TMZ resistance, quiescence).

## 4. Therapy Relevance

**Modeled therapy:**
- ACT (adoptive cell transfer) therapy — injection of antigen-specific T-cells.
- The therapy effect is modeled as an additional death rate for cancer cells: `t(z, p) · K⁻¹` per T-cell of type z per cancer cell of phenotype p.

**Therapy protocols explored:**
- Single-specificity T-cell therapy (one type of T-cell, targeting differentiated cells only).
- Two-specificity T-cell therapy (second T-cell type added, targeting dedifferentiated cells).
- Different initial T-cell doses modeled.

**Key finding:**
- Single-specificity therapy reliably fails due to phenotypic switching and stochastic T-cell extinction.
- Two-specificity therapy achieves cure in almost all stochastic simulations.
- Stochastic effects are crucial: deterministic model predicts convergence to a stable coexistence fixed point, but stochastic system frequently shows T-cell extinction and relapse.

**Radiotherapy / TMZ / combined:** Not modeled in this paper. Framework is general and can accommodate these by substituting therapy agents.

## 5. Mathematical / Modeling Relevance

**State space:**
- Finite trait space `X = G × P ⊍ Z ⊍ W`, where G = genotypes, P = phenotypes, Z = T-cell types, W = cytokine types.
- Population at time t: measure `ν_t^K = (1/K) Σ ν_t(x) δ_x`.

**CTMC structure:**
- Each individual has exponential clocks for: birth (with/without mutation), natural death, competition death, therapy death, phenotypic switch.
- Rates depend on the current population state (density-dependent).
- The process is a continuous-time Markov chain on a countable state space (population counts).

**Key transition rates per cell of type (g, p):**
- Natural birth rate: `b(p)`
- Natural death rate: `d(p)`
- Competition death rate: `c(p, p̃) · K⁻¹` (per competitor of phenotype p̃)
- Birth-reducing competition: `c_b(p, p̃) · K⁻¹`
- Therapy death rate: `t(z, p) · K⁻¹` (per T-cell of type z)
- Natural switch rate: `s^g(p, p̃)` (phenotype p → p̃)
- Cytokine-induced switch rate: `s_w^g(p, p̃) · K⁻¹` (per cytokine of type w)

**Mean-field ODE limit:**
- As K → ∞, the rescaled process converges a.s. to a system of quadratic ODEs.
- The ODE system is: `ṅ_x = (birth - death - competition - therapy) · n_x ± switch terms`.
- Mutations are invisible in the deterministic limit when mutation probabilities scale as `μ_g ~ K⁻¹` → 0.

**Gillespie / SSA:**
- The paper uses stochastic simulation (Gillespie algorithm) throughout.
- The hybrid stochastic-deterministic approach is discussed in Kraut 2021 (companion piece).

**Fixed point analysis:**
- Multiple fixed points exist; stability analysis in the ODE system guides interpretation of stochastic outcomes.
- Metastability: the stochastic system can transition between regions near different fixed points.

## 6. Parameters and Quantitative Information

The paper explicitly states that parameters in the theoretical examples are "chosen ad hoc to highlight the influence of randomness." For the biologically calibrated simulation (Fig. 4):

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| K (carrying capacity) | 10⁵ | Scaling parameter for stochastic simulation |
| Initial T-cell dose (n_{z_x}(0)) | 0.02 or 0.2 (rescaled) | Two scenarios |
| Relapse time (differentiated) | ~90 days after therapy | From comparison to experimental data |
| Relapse time (dedifferentiated) | ~190 days after therapy | From comparison to experimental data |
| Remission duration | ~50–60 days | From comparison to experimental data |
| Tumour diameter at diagnosis | ~8 mm | From Landsberg et al. experimental data |

All parameter values in the theoretical examples are ad hoc. For quantitative simulation, they were fit to the Landsberg melanoma mouse model — not to GBM. **These specific values should not be transferred to GBM without justification.**

## 7. Assumptions and Limitations

- Applied to melanoma, not GBM. Biological transfer requires explicit assumptions.
- The model does not include spatial structure.
- Mutations are modeled but assumed rare (scaled to vanish in the deterministic limit).
- T-cell exhaustion is not explicitly modeled — it is implicitly captured in the T-cell death rate.
- The model does not include pharmacokinetics (drug concentration dynamics over time).
- Parameters are either ad hoc or fit to a single mouse experiment — not transferable without re-fitting.
- The paper focuses on immune escape via phenotypic switching; chemoresistance (e.g., TMZ) involves different mechanisms.

## 8. Possible Use in This Project

- **Use in version 1:** The CTMC framework (trait space, exponential clocks, Q-matrix construction, carrying capacity scaling) is the direct mathematical template for the GBM model implementation. The mean-field ODE derivation (K → ∞) justifies the parallel deterministic approximation module. The concept of phenotypic switching maps to S ↔ Q (dormancy) and P ↔ D transitions.
- **Use for documentation:** The metastability analysis and fixed-point interpretation are useful for explaining why stochastic behavior near extinction differs qualitatively from the ODE prediction.
- **Background only:** The immunotherapy application details (T-cells, TNF-α, ACT) are not directly applicable to GBM. The Landsberg melanoma parameter values must not be reused.

## 9. Modeling Interpretation

> **[INTERPRETATION — not a source fact]**
> The Baar et al. framework is the mathematical blueprint for the new project. Key decisions it supports: (1) use a rescaled CTMC where population sizes are divided by K, (2) include density-dependent death rates via a competition term, (3) model therapy as an additional death rate proportional to drug concentration, (4) implement phenotypic switching as a rate-based transition between states, (5) derive the ODE limit formally to cross-validate stochastic simulations. The paper's central warning — that deterministic models fail precisely when populations drop low and stochastic extinction matters — is the scientific justification for using a stochastic model in the first place.
